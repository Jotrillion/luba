History

Open each chapter to explore the Luba story, from the early Upemba settlements to the kingdom's expansion and legacy.

Origins and foundations

Archaeological research shows that the Upemba Depression had been occupied continuously since at least the 4th century AD. By the 6th century, fishing people lived on lakeshores, worked iron, and traded palm oil. By the 10th century, the people of Upemba diversified their economy, combining fishing, farming, and metal-working.

Metal-workers relied on traders to bring copper and charcoal for smelting. Traders exported salt and iron items, and imported glass beads and cowry shells from the distant Indian Ocean. Over time, social stratification and governance began to form, and the centre of the empire in the Kabongo region emerged as a key area for further research.

Oral traditions and royal lineages

Luba-Katanga oral traditions describe the lineage of Kyubaka Ubaka and Kibumba Bumba, and trace the movement westward into the lands of the Luba. Figures such as Nkongolo, Mbidi Kiluwe, and Kalala Ilunga anchor the stories of sacred kingship, conflict, and the rise of Ilunga's court at Mwilunde.

These narratives, recorded in the early 20th century, emphasize the sacred role of leaders, the power of ritual symbols, and the formation of dynasties through alliances, contests, and succession struggles.

Empire, government, and memory

The Luba kingdom developed a durable form of government led by the Mulopwe (sacred king) and a court of nobles known as Bamfumus. Kings ruled through clan leaders while the Bambudye secret society preserved the memory of the kingdom's rulers and customs.

The Mbudye tradition links rulers to Kalala Ilunga, a revered hunter and culture hero credited with introducing advanced iron forging techniques. Villages and shrines dedicated to royal legacies dotted the Luba heartland.

Economy, arts, and beliefs

Luba traders connected the Congolese forest to the mineral-rich Copperbelt region. A tribute system redistributed agricultural, hunting, and mining resources, while the ruling class held a monopoly on trade items such as salt, copper, and iron ore.

The arts held high status, with Luba staffs, headrests, and royal seats featuring female figures linked to spiritual and ancestral power. Beliefs in prophetic dreams and ancestor worship shaped artistic forms, while the kasala poetic tradition carried myths, proverbs, and historical narratives.

Expansion and decline

From 1700 to 1860, the Luba expanded their influence through tributaries and the establishment of "fire kingdoms" on frontier regions. Expansion emphasized tribute and trade rather than direct conquest.

In the late 19th century, long-distance trade and external raids destabilized the kingdom. The Luba Kingdom fractured after succession disputes in 1889 and later became part of the Congo Free State.
